Medicare 101

Understanding Medicare in 2026: A Complete Guide for New Enrollees

By Ally Valdez February 24, 2026 8 min read

Introduction: Welcome to Medicare

Turning 65 is a milestone moment in life—and it comes with a significant decision: how to navigate your healthcare coverage through Medicare. Whether you're about to reach this age or are already considering your options, the amount of information available can feel overwhelming. Between Parts A, B, C, and D, enrollment periods, premium costs, and the choice between Original Medicare and Medicare Advantage, it's easy to feel lost.

This comprehensive guide is designed to simplify Medicare for you. We'll walk through everything you need to know to make informed decisions about your coverage, understand important deadlines, and avoid costly mistakes that many new enrollees overlook.

What Is Medicare? Understanding the Four Parts

Medicare is a federal health insurance program primarily for people age 65 and older, though some younger individuals with disabilities or specific conditions may also qualify. The program is divided into four distinct parts, each covering different healthcare services:

Medicare Part A: Hospital Insurance

Part A covers inpatient hospital care, skilled nursing facility care, hospice care, and some home health services. Most people pay no monthly premium for Part A because they or their spouse paid payroll taxes while working. However, you will pay a deductible ($1,676 in 2026) and coinsurance costs when you use hospital services.

Key Fact About Part A

Part A is typically automatic when you turn 65. You don't usually need to enroll, but you should verify your coverage through your Social Security account online.

Medicare Part B: Medical Insurance

Part B covers doctor visits, outpatient care, diagnostic tests, and preventive services. You pay a monthly premium (currently $189.40 for most beneficiaries in 2026), an annual deductible ($240 in 2026), and 20% coinsurance for most services after you meet your deductible.

Part B enrollment requires action during your Initial Enrollment Period (IEP), which begins three months before your 65th birthday and extends three months after. Missing this deadline can result in a permanent surcharge on your premiums.

Medicare Part D: Prescription Drug Coverage

Part D helps pay for prescription medications and is offered by private insurance companies approved by Medicare. While enrollment is optional, it's important to sign up when you first become eligible. If you don't enroll when first eligible and go without coverage for 63 days or more, you may owe a late enrollment penalty for as long as you have Part D coverage.

Medicare Part C: Medicare Advantage

Part C (also called Medicare Advantage) is an alternative to Original Medicare Parts A and B. Private insurance companies offer these plans, which must cover everything that Original Medicare covers—but often include prescription drug coverage (Part D), dental, vision, and hearing benefits. The tradeoff is restricted provider networks and higher out-of-pocket costs in some cases.

Original Medicare vs. Medicare Advantage vs. Medigap: Finding Your Best Fit

One of the most important decisions you'll make is choosing between Original Medicare with a Medigap supplement or a Medicare Advantage plan. Each approach has distinct advantages:

Original Medicare (Parts A & B) + Medigap

  • Greater provider flexibility: You can see any doctor or specialist who accepts Medicare without prior authorization
  • Nationwide coverage: Original Medicare works the same everywhere in the country
  • Financial predictability: Medigap plans limit your out-of-pocket exposure
  • Higher premiums: You pay for Parts A, B, and D separately, plus a Medigap premium

Medicare Advantage (Part C)

  • All-in-one coverage: Usually includes Part A, Part B, and Part D, plus extras like dental and vision
  • Lower monthly premiums: Many plans have $0 premiums for Parts A and B
  • Network restrictions: You must use in-network doctors (except for emergency services)
  • Prior authorization requirements: Specialists and certain procedures may require approval

Consider Your Healthcare Needs

If you see many specialists or travel frequently, Original Medicare with Medigap may suit you better. If you prefer lower premiums and integrated coverage with dental/vision benefits, Medicare Advantage could be ideal. Take time to review plan details and compare your potential costs under each scenario.

Key Enrollment Dates and Deadlines for 2026

Missing Medicare enrollment deadlines can be costly—literally, in the form of permanent surcharges on your premiums. Here are the critical dates to mark on your calendar:

Initial Enrollment Period (IEP)

If you're turning 65 or becoming newly eligible, your Initial Enrollment Period starts three months before your birthday month and ends three months after. For example, if your birthday is in June, your IEP runs from March through August. You must enroll in Part B during this window to avoid a late penalty.

Annual Enrollment Period (AEP)

From October 15 to December 7 each year, all Medicare beneficiaries can review their coverage and make changes. This is the time to switch between Original Medicare and Medicare Advantage, change Medigap plans, or adjust prescription drug coverage.

Medicare Advantage Disenrollment Period

If you're in a Medicare Advantage plan, you have an additional window (January 1-31) to drop your MA plan and return to Original Medicare without penalty.

Mark Your Calendar

October 15 - December 7: Annual Enrollment Period — Review and change plans
January 1-31: MA Disenrollment Period — Leave MA plans if needed

Common Mistakes New Medicare Enrollees Make

Learning from the experiences of others can help you navigate your own Medicare journey successfully. Here are the top mistakes we see new enrollees make:

Mistake #1: Assuming You're Automatically Enrolled

Many people think Medicare enrollment happens automatically at 65. While Part A is largely automatic if you're receiving Social Security, Part B is not. Failing to enroll in Part B (or declining it) by mistake can trigger permanent late-enrollment penalties. Always take action to confirm your enrollment status.

Mistake #2: Not Enrolling in Part D When First Eligible

Even if you don't currently take prescription medications, enrolling in Part D when you become eligible protects you from future penalties. The cost of not enrolling can add up significantly if you start medications later.

Mistake #3: Choosing a Plan Without Reviewing Your Medications

If you take regular prescriptions, always check whether your preferred medications are covered under a plan's formulary before enrolling. What's covered and at what tier (cost level) varies widely between plans.

Mistake #4: Ignoring Your Annual Enrollment Period

Your healthcare needs and plan options change each year. New plans may offer better coverage, and your current plan's costs may increase. Reviewing your options during AEP ensures you're still in the best plan for your situation.

Mistake #5: Not Asking Questions

Medicare can be complex, and there's no such thing as a "dumb question." Don't hesitate to ask your doctor, a Medicare counselor, or insurance agent to clarify how a plan works before you enroll.
